Porcelain
How do you make porcelain? There is a surprisingly simple logic to formulating them and to adjusting their working, drying, glazing and firing properties for different purposes.
Key phrases linking here:
mullite porcelains, mullite porcelain, porcelain
 -
Learn more
Details
A "
mullite porcelain
" pottery mug and an "
anorthite porcelain
" cup.
Traditional utilitarian porcelains are comparatively white-burning and
vitreous
clay bodies
that are normally made from
feldspar
, clay and quartz (vs
bone china
made from bone ash, feldspar and
kaolin
). During firing, the feldspar flows and dissolves many of the other particles into an increasingly viscous glassy melt that bonds the quartz particles and, and if temperatures are sufficient, provides the conditions to enable the flat kaolin crystals to convert to long
mullite
crystals (vs anorthite crystals in bone china).
Mullite porcelains
normally have a smooth and pleasant surface (if fired to sufficient temperatures). Those low in
titanium
and
vitrified
well can be
translucent
.
Porcelain
clay bodies are made from fine-grained materials and lack the iron impurities of stonewares. They are usually fired above 1180C, the lower practical limit of feldspar as a vitreous body
flux
. Porcelains tend to warp during firing because they must be taken closer to the melting point to achieve the desired properties.
Mullite porcelains can be made to a wide range of plasticities. Plastic porcelains have traditionally been much shorter (less plastic) than their
stoneware
or
earthenware
counterparts. But in recent years white-burning bentonites have made it possible to make even translucent porcelains plastic (those used by potters are much more plastic than those used in industry for machine forming). Porcelain casting slips have historically achieved the whitest and most translucent results because they do not need to be as plastic (the plastic materials contribute the most iron, which darkens the color, and titanium, which impedes
translucency
). But in recent years, the advent of micro-fine plasticizers that do double-duty as translucency
mechanism
catalysts, actually make it possible to make a plastic body that is more translucent. Typical such porcelains are a mix of clays (kaolin,
ball clay
and plasticizers), feldspar (the melter) and quartz (the
low-expansion
filler and framework).
Photo not found
or not published
By employing
frits
instead of feldspar, it is possible to achieve fired hardness at much lower temperatures.
Frit
can be used to create a glass matrix that fuses and bonds particles at their points of contact. Like the
feldspars
in porcelain, the glass can also fill the voids and seal the matrix from the penetration of water. Although not really a porcelain, such bodies can be surprising able to resist
warping during firing
, develop excellent strength and survive fast firing.
Electrical insulator aluminous porcelains are an hybrid. Feldspar bonds a microstructure dominated by angular, size-controlled, alumina grains. A small amount of kaolin forms some mullite. The result is a matrix having much better mechanical and dielectric strength, insulating properties and resistance to
thermal shock
. Although the material cost of bodies, whose main ingredient is
calcined alumina
,  other production costs dilute that. The added cost of alumina is thus acceptable given the performance gains.
A starting recipe for tranditional cone 10 porcelain is 50% clay (a mix of kaolin and ball clay) and 25% each of feldspar and
silica
- the so-called “25 Porcelain” (this recipe assumes a relatively pure kaolin and a potent feldspar). The less kaolin (or less plastic clean kaolin) in the clay portion of the recipe the less plastic the body will be (but the whiter it will fire). Ball clay can be omitted if a
plasticizer
(like hectorite, smectite or
bentonite
) assists the kaolin (typically 5% or less). By switching the feldspar to
nepheline syenite
,
maturity
can be dropped a little. Increasing the feldspar to 35% can enable maturity at
cone 6
(35% is the practical limit to allow enough space in the recipe for the needed silica and clay). Reducing the feldspar to 15% can raise the temperature needed for maturity to cone 12. Substituting a high-soda low-boron frit (e.g. Ferro 3110), for some or all of the feldspar, can drop the temperature as low as
cone 06
. Be aware that reducing silica too much (e.g. down to 15%) could mean that glazes will
craze
. The most extreme example of a cost-is-no-object super-white porcelain is one that employs a zircon-opacified frit as the flux and a highly processed smectite as the plasticizer.
For
maximum density
(not always needed) porcelains should be fired to the temperature at which the
porosity
and
fired shrinkage
curves reach their minimum and maximum (respectively), or a little beyond that. If they are fired higher than that, they become too unstable in the kiln. Development projects to create and maintain a porcelain recipe should do maturity testing at several cones above and below the intended firing temperature to get a complete view of the body's performance (use the
SHAB test
procedure).
In recent years a myriad of high-technology products have been created that further stretch the traditional term "porcelain", especially for 3D printing; these are beyond the scope of this website.

Two reasons why porcelain recipes need silica
This picture has its own page with more detail,
click here
to see it.
This is 70%
kaolin
and 30%
feldspar
. Fired at
cone 6
with glaze
G2926B
. The fired body has a nice porcelaneous surface. But, right out of the kiln, it crazes like this! The dense
craze
pattern indicates a very serious fit problem. The
thermal expansion
of the kaolin:feldspar mix is much too low. Adding 25%
low-expansion
silica
will solve the problem. The other issue is with the flat particle shape of kaolin. The
throwing
process has lined up the predominant kaolin particles concentric to the centre. During drying, and especially firing, more shrinkage occurs across them than along them. All ten of the cups made cracked like this! The solution is adding a filler, one with rounded particles to separate the kaolin plates. Silica is perfect, using the same 25% addition. The grains act like aggregate in concrete, strengthening the matrix and separating the clay particles, forcing them to orient more randomly.

Do not rely on material data sheets, do the testing
