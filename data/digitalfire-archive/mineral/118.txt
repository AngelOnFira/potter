Feldspar
An indispensable material in the ceramic industry. Most ceramic bodies employ
feldspar
as a
flux
to vitrify them at a lower temperature (the feldspar creates a glass that glues the more
refractory
particles together to form the fired matrix). Most medium and
high-temperature glazes
employ it as a flux. They melt, but not too fast, and they help form a durable glass.
Feldspars
are naturally occurring
crystalline
rocks that melt and cool to form a glass (nature cooled them slowly (devitrified them) to form crystalline minerals). There are many kinds of feldspars, but in
ceramics
, soda, potash and lime feldspars are the most common (technically known as plagioclase, orthoclase and anorthite).
Porcelains can contain up to 50% feldspar,
stoneware
bodies around 15%. Feldspar sources
K
2
O
and
Na
2
O
to glazes, these can produce very brilliant results. However, the high
KNaO
content produces high
thermal expansion
, which in turn produces
crazing
. Thus, it is important to control the amount of feldspar and employ materials that source other
low-expansion
oxides
like
CaO
,
MgO
,
Li
2
O
,
BaO
,
SrO
, etc.
