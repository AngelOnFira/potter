Surface Tension
In ceramics, surface tension is discussed in two contexts: The glaze melt and the glaze suspension. In both, the quality of the glaze surface is impacted.

surface tension
 -
Learn more
Details
In
ceramics
, the issue of
surface tension
is most evident in two areas: The glaze melt and
slurry
rheology
.
Surface Tension in Glaze Melts
When a glaze is fired, it melts to form a molten silicate glass. This melt exhibits surface tension, a thermodynamic property that reflects the tendency of the liquid to minimize its
surface area
. In ceramic glazes, surface tension plays a key role in how
the melt
wets the
clay body
, releases gases, and levels during firing and cooling.
Wetting and Spreading: Low surface tension promotes wetting of the clay body and allows the glaze to spread more readily across the surface. High surface tension resists wetting and encourages the melt to contract into thicker regions, contributing to edge pull-back, beading,
crawling
, and poor coverage.
Bubble Release: Assuming sufficient melt
viscosity
, surface tension governs how easily gas bubbles rupture at the melt surface. Low surface tension enhances merging and makes bubble films easier to break. High surface tension strengthens the bubble skin, allowing bubbles to persist, reseal, or remain trapped.
Levelling and Surface Smoothness: Surface tension provides the driving force for surface smoothing. However, the extent and rate of levelling are controlled primarily by viscosity (melt mobility). A glaze with high surface tension but low viscosity can still level well, while a glaze with low surface tension but high viscosity may freeze before smoothing is complete. Surface tension helps explain why some glazes pull away from sharp edges while pooling in recesses.
Surface tension in silicate melts is strongly influenced by oxide
chemistry
, as different
oxides
alter the structure and ionic bonding of the glass network. A critical caution: many oxides affect surface tension indirectly by changing viscosity and melt structure at the same time, so practical glaze behavior cannot be predicted from surface tension alone.
Oxides commonly associated with higher surface tension (assuming similar melt viscosity, approximate, system- and concentration-dependent). Rightward have higher surface tension and are thus associated with increased melt stiffness, reduced wetting and bubble mobility:
MgO
,
Al
2
O
3
,
ZrO2
,
ZnO
,
CaO
,
SnO2
,
BaO
,
SrO
Oxides commonly associated with lower surface tension (lowest surface tension rightward; these generally promote wetting, improve levelling, enhance bubble merging and rupture, more gloss):
PbO
,
B
2
O
3
,
K
2
O
,
Na
2
O
,
Li
2
O
SiO
2
forms the glass network and usually raises viscosity strongly, but its effect on surface tension is moderate rather than extreme.
Fe
2
O
3
effects vary strongly with oxidation state, concentration and firing atmosphere.
ZnO is chemically distinctive in that it can function as a strong
flux
while also increasing melt viscosity, modifying surface behavior and contributing to matte or semi-matte effects in some systems.
ZnO is unusual because it often acts as a flux while simultaneously stiffening melt behavior in certain systems. It can increase
crystallization
tendency, promote matte surfaces and alter bubble persistence. Its effect is highly composition-dependent.
Practical Implications:
High surface tension + high viscosity: Beading, crawling, poor bubble escape,
pinholing
, incomplete healing, matte or uneven surfaces.
Low surface tension + low viscosity: Good wetting, smooth levelling, glossy surfaces, good defect healing, possible running.
High surface tension + low viscosity: Can still level well, but may show edge pullback, thick/thin variation, and bubble persistence.
Low surface tension + high viscosity: Implies good coverage, satin surfaces, incomplete levelling if cooling is rapid.
Surface tension is strongly implicated in glaze
blistering
.
Blisters
happen where the molten glaze resists bubble rupture at the surface. These or their unhealed remnants often survive to the fired piece when gas pressure inflates the bubble, surface tension maintains the film and viscosity prevents rapid healing after rupture. Strangely, glazes of a more
fluid melt
can exhibit this problem more, the melt is mobile enough for bubbles to expand, but the surface remains strong enough to hold them. The solution can be a slower cooling through the range at which increasing temperature-induced viscosity has the power to rupture the film. Replacing some higher-surface-tension oxides with lower-surface-tension fluxes often improves
blister
resistance more effectively than simply increasing
melt fluidity
.
Surface Tension of Glaze Slurries
On flat surfaces,
glaze laydown
is not normally an issue, but slurries are suspensions and can resist adhesion and give poor coverage on irregular surfaces (e.g. acute angles,
incised
or stamped decoration or labelling). Surfactants are available to reduce the surface tension of aqueous glaze systems, they deagglomerate particles and help their homogeneous dispersion over the surface. Polyethylene glycol and Glycerin are considered wetting agents also.

calcium carbonate
can cause
blistering
as it decomposes during a fast firing in our electric kiln. This is a

The difference that caused blistering: Firing schedule!
This picture has its own page with more detail,
click here
to see it.
These are the same glaze, same thickness, Ulexite-based G2931B glaze, fired to cone 03 on a
terra cotta
body. The one on the right was fired from 1850F to 1950F at 100F/hr, then soaked 15 minutes and shut off. The problem is
surface tension
. Like soapy water, when this glaze reaches cone 03
the melt
is quite fluid. Since there is
decomposition
happening within the body, gases being generated vent out through surface pores and blow bubbles. I could soak at cone 03 as long as I wanted and the bubbles would just sit there. The one on the left was fired to 100F below cone 03, soaked half an hour (to clear micro-bubble clouds), then at 108F/hr to cone 03 and soaked 30 minutes, then control-cooled at 108F/hr to 1500F. During this cool, at some point well below cone 03, the increasing
viscosity
of the melt becomes sufficient to overcome the surface tension and break the bubbles. If that point is not traversed too quickly, the glaze has a chance to smooth out (using whatever remaining fluidity the melt has). Ideally I should identify exactly where that is and soak there for a while.
